Radar Charts & Comparative Analysis
The "theory" here is Pattern Recognition.
The Second Dataset: Humans are much faster at seeing the difference between two shapes (overlays) than comparing two lists of numbers. By adding a second dataset, you create a "delta" (the gap between the two shapes) which immediately highlights strengths and weaknesses.
The "Dark Theme" Factor: In a Radar chart, lines often overlap. A dark background allows you to use additive color blending or high-contrast neon strokes that make the intersections readable rather than a muddy mess.



Horizontal Bar Charts & Readability
This is primarily about Cognitive Load and Typography.
Scanning Patterns: We read left-to-right. In a vertical bar chart, long labels (like "TypeScript" or "Python") often have to be rotated 45 or 90 degrees. This forces the brain to "re-orient," which slows down comprehension. Horizontal bars allow the text to sit naturally next to the data.
Linear Ranking: Horizontal charts are the gold standard for "Top 10" lists because they mimic a finish line, making the "winner" at the top feel intuitive.



Per-Bar Accent Colors (Visual Encoding)
This uses a concept called Identity Encoding.
Categorical Distinction: Instead of using one color for the whole chart, giving each language its "brand" color (e.g., blue for TypeScript, yellow for JS) creates a mental shortcut. The user stops reading the label after a while and just identifies the color.
Aesthetic Cohesion: On a dark theme, these accent colors act as "light sources," guiding the eye to the most important data points without overwhelming the UI.



Comparison Mode & Occlusion
The biggest challenge with Radar charts is Occlusion (one shape hiding another).
Theory: To fix this, you likely used low-opacity fills or stroke-only rendering for the second dataset. This allows the user to see "through" the top layer to the data beneath it.
